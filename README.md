# stack-overflow-survey

## Project Objective
The goal of this project is to explore the multifaceted profile of today’s IT professional through big data analysis. We sought to answer key industry questions: how people learn, what technologies they choose, where they work and how much they earn, taking into account various demographic and professional factors.

## Dataset Used
Source: Results of the annual Stack Overflow survey (the most representative survey of developers worldwide).  
Scope: Tens of thousands of respondents and over 120 metrics.  
Features: The data includes both technical information (technology stack) and socio-economic information (salaries, employment type, education).

## Key Questions
For our analysis, we selected several key areas:  

- Education: What percentage of professionals choose online courses as their primary path into the profession?
- Technology: How popular is Python among different age groups?
- Economics: What are the income levels of Python developers in different countries and industries?
- Work format: How popular is remote work, and where do “top performers” (highly paid professionals) work?
- Experience: What are the measures of central tendency in the modern industry?

## Process
The work was divided into the following technical processing stages:

1. Data Cleaning: Checking data for completeness. I found that due to the large number of questions, no respondent answered all 126 items, which required us to use flexible filtering for each individual query.

2. Filtration & Segmenting: Identifying target groups (e.g., “Python developers,” “Remote workers”).

3. Statistical Analysis: Calculating means, medians, and quartiles (Q3) for salary analysis.

4. Aggregation: Grouping data by country, age and education level to identify patterns.

## Project Insights
Python's popularity: 37.5% of respondents use Python. At the same time, we are seeing a high adoption rate of the language across different age groups.

Educational trend: A huge number of people (nearly 11,000 in the sample) learned to code specifically through online courses, which calls into question the dominance of traditional education.

Remote work: Remote work has become the norm—over 10,000 respondents work remotely, and this format correlates with high income levels in certain industries.

Salary anomalies: I discovered an interesting insight - the highest salaries (Top 5) do not always go to those with master’s degrees. The top ranks include professionals with associate degrees, underscoring the importance of skills over a degree.

## Final Conclusion

This project has demonstrated that the modern IT market is inclusive and dynamic.

Skills matter more than degrees: High salaries correlate with experience and proficiency in in-demand programming languages (such as Python), rather than solely with the level of academic education.

Flexibility: Remote work has become an integral part of a successful career.

Continuous learning: The high percentage of online course usage confirms the concept of lifelong learning.
